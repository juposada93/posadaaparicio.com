AI-assisted teams outperform AI-led teams but not human-only teams in assessing research reproducibility in quantitative social science

With A. Brodeur, D. Valenta, A. Marcoci, D. Mikola, B. Barbarioli, et al.

Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 123(22), e2524747123

Peer-reviewed publication

DOI: 10.1073/pnas.2524747123

288 researchers in 103 teams reproduced published social science with no AI, AI assistance, or AI in the lead: human-only and AI-assisted teams reproduced 94% and 91% of results, AI-led teams only 37%.

Expert human judgment is still indispensable for verification — but a 37% autonomous rate suggests AI can help where human review doesn't scale.

Peacefully Demobilizing Rebels: Identity, Emotional Cues, and the FARC

With Michael Jetter and Christopher Parsons

IZA Discussion Paper No. 16054

Under journal review (IZA Discussion Paper)

Radio messages aired during national-team football matches persuaded FARC fighters to demobilize: family-themed appeals brought home more than 1,000 rebels, while national-unity appeals did not.

Persuasion worked when identity and emotional cues aligned — messages landed hardest right after unexpected losses.

Published 2022

Captivating news: Media attention and FARC kidnappings

With Michael Jetter

Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization, 202, 69-81

Peer-reviewed publication

DOI: 10.1016/j.jebo.2022.07.029

News coverage of a proposed prisoner exchange shifted FARC kidnappings away from ransom targets and toward political hostages the group could trade.

Media attention itself — not slant or new information — changed the strategic value of targets, also raising killings of politicians while reducing other attacks.

The Wrong Snowball: Reintegration Signals and Demobilization in Colombia

With Verónica Abril

Working paper

Working paper; draft available on request

When demobilized ex-combatants opened state-funded businesses, nearby demobilizations fell — about 734 fewer over three years, or 5.1 percent of FARC demobilizations in 2005-2016.

Reintegration's visibility can backfire: when post-exit life looks fragile or unsafe, each public milestone discourages the next defector.

You Speak, We Shoot: On the Unintended Consequences of Community Radios on Colombia's Violent Conflict

With Gustavo Canavire-Bacarreza and Eric Neumayer

Working paper

Under journal review

Introducing community radios in Colombia increased killings by right-wing paramilitaries (AUC) but not by the FARC — and FARC violence even fell over time in more left-leaning municipalities.

Giving communities a public voice threatened the armed group whose control it challenged: media interventions can backfire where violent actors dominate.
